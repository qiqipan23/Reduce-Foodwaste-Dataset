Machine Learning Solution (in plain English)

Our system predicts how many items will sell on a given day so stores can plan production more accurately. It uses patterns from past sales and combines them with signals like weekday, holidays, and weather.

What it does

Estimates daily sales for each store before the day starts.

What it uses

Past sales + calendar patterns + holiday signals + weather conditions.

Why it helps

Better planning means fewer unsold items and fewer stockouts.

Key takeaway

You don’t need to understand machine learning to use the output: the forecast is simply a best estimate of tomorrow’s demand based on what has happened in similar situations before.

How it works

We follow a simple process: collect useful signals, learn patterns from history, and generate a forecast.

1) Collect signals

  • Weekday and season (e.g., weekends vs weekdays)
  • Holidays and special days
  • Weather (temperature, sunshine, precipitation)
  • Recent demand (sales last week / last 14 days)

2) Learn patterns

The model learns which situations usually lead to higher or lower sales by looking at many past examples. For instance, it can learn that weekends are typically busier, or that certain weather conditions change buying behavior.

3) Output a forecast

  • Predicted daily sales (per store, per day)
  • Comparison across models for reliability
  • Forecasts that support waste-aware planning

Models we compare

We test three well-known methods and choose the one that is most accurate and reliable on future dates. Think of them as different “forecasting engines” trained on the same information.

Random Forest

Best for: a stable baseline that performs well without heavy tuning.

Trade-off: may miss some subtle patterns in demand.

LightGBM

Best for: strong accuracy on business-style tables (sales, weather, holidays).

Trade-off: needs careful settings to avoid overfitting.

XGBoost

Best for: a strong benchmark model that often performs very well.

Trade-off: can be slower and benefits from tuning.

What information goes into the forecast

Inputs (features)

  • Date & calendar: weekday, week, month, year, weekend indicator
  • Store: store identifier (different stores can have different demand levels)
  • Holidays: school/state holidays and special days
  • Weather: temperature, sunshine, precipitation
  • Recent sales history: lagged sales and rolling averages (e.g., last week)

Why sales history matters

The strongest clue for tomorrow is often what happened recently. Lag and rolling-average features help the model capture momentum and weekly patterns without needing any manual rules.

How we test it (so it’s fair)

We test on future dates

We train on earlier periods and evaluate on later periods, similar to how real forecasting works.

We repeat the test

We use time-series splits (walk-forward validation) to check stability across different time ranges.

We report error clearly

Metrics like MAE/RMSE summarize how far predictions are from actual sales on average.

Metric meanings (simple)

  • MAE: average absolute difference between prediction and actual sales
  • RMSE: like MAE, but it penalizes large errors more

FAQ

Do I need to understand machine learning to use this?

No. The output is simply a forecasted sales number (and we compare models to choose the most reliable one).

Does this eliminate food waste completely?

No, but it helps reduce avoidable overproduction by making planning closer to expected demand.

Why do you use three models?

Different models can behave differently across time and stores. Comparing them helps us choose a model that is accurate and consistent.
